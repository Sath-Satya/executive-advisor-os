## Discovery Meeting

*SponAI Consulting &nbsp;|&nbsp; [Prospect Name] &nbsp;|&nbsp; April 2026*

This conversation belongs to you. We are here to listen and learn before we recommend anything.

<!-- Speaker notes:
The opening line of this deck — "This conversation belongs to you" — is the most important thing you will say in the first 90 seconds. Discovery meetings fail when the seller treats them as a pitch in disguise. Start by acknowledging that explicitly. The tone you are setting is: we are consultants, not vendors. We ask questions, we listen, and then we determine whether and how we can help. Before the meeting, research the prospect for 20–30 minutes. Read their most recent press releases, check LinkedIn for the stakeholders in the room, review their annual report or public filings if available. Look for three things: stated strategic priorities, recent organizational changes, and any public pain signals (earnings calls often contain these). Have at least five specific questions ready that show you did your homework. "We noticed you announced a new value-based care initiative last month — can you tell us more about where that stands?" is infinitely better than "Tell me about your organization."
-->

## Questions for You

# What We Want to Understand Today

- **Operations:** Where does your team spend the most time on tasks that feel manual, repetitive, or error-prone?
- **Technology:** What are the 1–2 systems your staff wishes worked differently — or worked together better?
- **Strategy:** What does success in your role look like 18 months from now — and what is in the way of getting there?

*We will take notes and follow up within 48 hours with a written summary of what we heard*

<!-- Speaker notes:
These three questions are designed to surface different categories of pain: operational friction, technology debt, and strategic blockers. Do not ask all three in rapid succession — ask the first one and then listen. Actively. Take notes. Ask follow-up questions. "Tell me more about that" and "How long has that been a problem?" are two of the most powerful phrases in discovery. The written follow-up commitment in the footnote is a differentiator — most sellers do not send a meeting summary. When you do, it signals professionalism, creates a shared record of the conversation, and gives you a natural reason to follow up. The summary is also valuable internally: it becomes the foundation of your qualification notes in Salesforce and informs the customization of any proposal you prepare later. Budget 20–25 minutes for this section of the meeting. If they start talking, let them talk. The more they share, the more you learn, and the better your proposal will be.

<!-- Speaker notes:

## Next Steps

# Where We Go From Here

- **48 hours:** We send you a written summary of today's conversation with our initial observations
- **1 week:** If there is a fit, we propose a 3-week POC scoped to your specific top-priority challenge
- **2 weeks:** POC kickoff — your environment, your data, your success criteria

*We will only propose a POC if we genuinely believe it will generate value for you. If the fit is not there, we will tell you.*

<!-- Speaker notes:
The "we will tell you if the fit is not there" line is one of the most trust-building things you can say in a discovery meeting. Most salespeople never say it — it sounds counterintuitive. But in enterprise sales, honesty about fit builds more pipeline than pressure does. If the fit truly is not there, saying so early protects your credibility for a future opportunity when the fit is better. The 48-hour written summary commitment creates a concrete, low-stakes next touchpoint that does not require the prospect to do anything. The POC proposal is the ask — not a purchase, not a commitment, just a proposal for three weeks of work. The threshold for "yes" is low. If they hesitate, ask: "What would you need to see in the POC to feel confident moving forward?" That question pre-qualifies the success criteria and gives you a clear target. End the meeting by asking for introductions to anyone else who should be part of the POC conversation — the VP of IT, a clinical champion, a data analyst. Expanding the contact map at discovery is far easier than doing it at proposal.

## The Ask

# One Decision — Before We Leave This Room

**Are we solving a real problem that is worth 3 weeks of your team's time to validate?**

If yes: &nbsp;*Let us schedule the POC kickoff call — 30 minutes, next week*

If you need more information: &nbsp;*Tell us what is missing — we will answer it in writing within 24 hours*

*[AE Name] &nbsp;|&nbsp; [email] &nbsp;|&nbsp; [phone] &nbsp;|&nbsp; SponAI Consulting*

<!-- Speaker notes:
The binary close at the end of a discovery deck is intentional. You are not asking for money, a contract, or a commitment to buy. You are asking one question: is this worth 3 weeks? That is a low-stakes decision. The two-option format gives the prospect a way to engage without committing: either they say yes to the kickoff, or they tell you what is missing. The second option is actually valuable — if they ask for more information, they are telling you exactly what their objection is, which is infinitely better than a non-response after the meeting. Before leaving the room, confirm the decision-maker's calendar availability for a kickoff call. If they say "send me an invite," send it before you are out of the parking lot. Speed of follow-through in discovery converts at measurably higher rates than follow-through that happens the next day. The discovery meeting is not just a conversation — it is an audition for how fast and responsive you will be as a partner.
-->
