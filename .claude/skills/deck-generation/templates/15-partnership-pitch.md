Partnership pitches differ from investor pitches in one critical way: both parties are evaluating each other. "We've spent two weeks studying your customer base, your product gaps, and your strategic priorities. We believe there is a $40M opportunity that neither organization can capture alone." The $40M figure should be real and defensible — break it down on slide 4. If the number is not yet validated, use "estimated" and flag it as a projection to be refined. ## Proposed Structure

# Channel Partnership — Simple Alignment, Clear Economics

- **Model:** Helix is sold as a certified add-on within [Partner]'s marketplace
- **Partner margin:** **22%** of Helix contract value for partner-sourced deals
- **Co-selling:** joint field team for opportunities &gt;$200K ACV
- **Co-marketing:** joint case studies, webinars, EHR user group presence

*Structure avoids channel conflict: partner owns the relationship; Helix owns support and delivery.*

<!-- Speaker notes:
The structure slide is where deals get made or lost. Be specific about the economics before you are asked. 22% channel margin is market rate for health IT channel partners — it signals you have done the research. The "partner owns the relationship" line is critical: many partnership pitches die because the incumbent partner fears being disintermediated. Explicitly stating that they own the customer relationship — and that Helix is in a support and delivery role — removes that fear. The co-selling threshold ($200K ACV) creates a natural escalation path for large deals without committing both sales teams to every small deal. If the partner asks for higher margin: "We modeled 22% because it preserves a healthy gross margin for Helix at scale. We are open to a tiered structure — 22% at standard volume, 27% at milestone volumes. Let's model it together." Having a tiered structure ready signals flexibility without desperation.
-->

*Assumes 6-month ramp for partner sales team training and certification.*

<!-- Speaker notes:
Three-scenario models signal analytical rigor. Every number should be defensible. Conservative case assumes 8 partner-sourced deals — that is less than one per quarter for the entire partner field team. It is intentionally very conservative: "Even in a scenario where the partnership underperforms, you generate $246K in the first year with zero investment from your engineering team." The base case is what you are genuinely targeting. The upside case assumes full adoption of co-selling and co-marketing. The joint pipeline number — $9M base — is distinct from closed revenue: it is what enters the pipeline through joint activities, and it is tracked monthly as a partnership health metric. The 6-month ramp footnote is honest: most channel partnerships take 3–6 months to produce revenue while the sales team learns the product. Do not overpromise Year 1.

## Timeline

# From Agreement to First Joint Close in 90 Days

| Phase | Activities | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| **Alignment** | NDA, term sheet, legal review | Weeks 1–3 |
| **Enablement** | Sales training, certification, co-marketing content | Weeks 4–8 |
| **Launch** | Partner portal live, first co-sell meeting | Week 9 |
| **First close** | Joint deal in pipeline &amp;gt;$150K ACV | Week 12 |
| **Quarterly review** | Pipeline, closed revenue, NPS | End of Q3 2026 |

<!-- Speaker notes:
A 90-day timeline to first joint close is ambitious but achievable — it signals that you are organized and have done this before. Walk through each phase. The legal phase (Weeks 1–3) is often the longest due to procurement and legal review at the partner organization. Flag it: "We have a template partnership agreement that we have used with two other channel partners. We can have it to your legal team by end of this week — that typically compresses the review cycle significantly." The enablement phase is critical: under-trained channel sales teams do not sell. Commit to a specific enablement package — live training, recorded modules, a certification quiz, a battle card. The first close at Week 12 is a soft commitment — it creates urgency without being a contractual penalty. If they miss it, it is a data point for the quarterly review, not a breach.
